What is a DDoS Attack?

A straightforward, human-friendly explanation of distributed denial-of-service attacks: what they are, how they affect systems, and approachable steps to reduce risk.

Quick definition

A DDoS (Distributed Denial of Service) attack is when many computers or devices send huge amounts of traffic or requests to a website, app, or network to overwhelm it. The result is that legitimate users can't use the service — it becomes slow or completely unreachable.

How it works (plain English)

  • Attackers direct lots of devices (commonly a botnet) to hit one target at the same time.
  • That flood can fill up bandwidth, crash services, or tie up server resources so real users can't get through.
  • Sometimes the traffic is random noise; sometimes it's crafted to exploit a weak part of the system.

Common flavours

  • Volumetric: massive amounts of data to saturate the network link.
  • Protocol: abusing the way network protocols work to exhaust servers or firewalls.
  • Application-layer: hitting specific app endpoints (like logins) so the app itself runs out of steam.

How to spot one

  • Your site becomes slow or times out for many users at once.
  • Traffic metrics spike dramatically from many different IPs or countries.
  • Monitoring tools show saturated links, growing queues, or suddenly high error rates.

Practical protection — what teams do

Below are practical, high-level protections commonly used by organisations. They’re deliberately non-technical so you can understand options when you speak with your host or security provider.

  • Use a CDN or a managed DDoS mitigation service to absorb and filter attack traffic before it reaches your origin servers.
  • Apply rate limits and simple filters at the edge to drop obviously malicious requests.
  • Run a Web Application Firewall (WAF) for application-layer protection and caching to reduce load.
  • Keep an incident response plan and contact your host or ISP immediately if you see signs of an attack.

What to do if you're hit

  • Contact your host or CDN immediately and give them timestamps and logs.
  • Follow their mitigation instructions — they can often route traffic through scrubbing centres quickly.
  • Avoid any retaliation. Retaliation is illegal and rarely helps.
